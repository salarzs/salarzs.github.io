Project 1

This study employs interface-resolved direct numerical simulations (DNS) using a conservative diffusive interface (CDI) method to analyze buoyancy-driven bubbly flow in a vertical water channel at a 2.7% air volume fraction. The simulations consider varying numbers of deformable air bubbles with fixed density and viscosity ratios, while systematically varying the Galilei and Eötvös numbers to examine the effects of buoyancy and surface tension forces. Results show that bubble distribution transitions from near-wall clustering at low Eötvös numbers (less deformable bubbles) to center-channel accumulation at high Eötvös numbers (more deformable bubbles), significantly altering flow patterns. Increased bubble deformability enhances central flow rates and reduces near-wall velocities, while higher Galilei numbers lead to greater flow velocities, intensified vorticity near walls, and higher energy dissipation. The study further explores turbulence characteristics, energy transfer mechanisms, and bubble size distributions, offering insights into the complex interactions between dispersed bubbles and the carrier fluid, with implications for industrial applications such as chemical mixing, oil-water separation, wastewater aeration, and nuclear cooling.
